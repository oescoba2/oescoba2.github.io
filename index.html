Lab Developer: Reinforcement Learning & Statistical Analysis (Dec 2023-June 2024)

The ACME program, paraphrasing one of its founders, was created to bring relevant 21st century math into a degree so that students would be ready to tackle modern problems. But ACME only has about 10 years of infancy, as such roadbumps would be encountered.

Serving under the student advisory council of ACME, SACME, (see below for that) I helped bring notice to the small lingering issues found within the course textbooks and lab materials that were not previously found. One of those first issues I voiced dealt with the material and problems a lab, called Sampling, meant to help us connect the content of Bayesian statistics we were taught. The original lab did not have clear connections to the material, so upon voicing the concern, I also offered to help fix it since I pitched in ideas based on what I had read from documentation of stats modules and material learned. Seeing that I also was doing very well in the material, I was then offered a teaching assistant job (normally offered after successful completion of junior year).

As a TA, I researched the documentation of SciPy regarding their stats module to see how we could implement Bayensian stats into the lab. Moreover, I also read other articles in the pedagogy of statistics as, at least while going through it, I had an idea of what we had learned but was not very confident in it. I am happy to say that I rewrote and rewrote countless of times the new material and statistics connection several times until it was reviewed and accepted by the graduate student TA. The new material contains an overview of statistics and explanation of parameter estimation. Moreover, it dives into Bayesian framework and theory as well as a comparison to that of Frequentist/Classical statistics. Lastly, I gave a working example to aid with the first two lab problems as well as provided further instruction on how to use PyMC for Bayesian work.

The next "big" issue I worked was towards the end of our junior year but also what we all considered a random lab given sometime during the first 3 weeks of the winter semester of junior year. The last lab of ACME volume 2 deals with Markov decision processes (MDPs) and stochastic optimization but in the scope of its main application: reinforcement learning (RL). I had voiced the concern that no one liked the lab as it seem to talk about material we were not taught (the theory of RL) but had an odd connections to MDPs. The lab also seemed fairly familiar to an earlier found in the ACME lab curriculum called Gymnasium that we all considered random as we never once again touched on the material, or so we thought.

The purpose of the Gymnasium lab is to introduce the Python Gymnasium model that is used for reinforcement learning. At least, the lab never did teach the theory and concepts behind reinforcement learning except just a quick definition since most of the lab dealt with working under certain environments. Then, when we come to the MDP lab at the very end of the junior core, we did not remember what we did in the Gymnasium lab. Thus, once my TA and friend, Jeddy, mentioned to me that the main idea being used was RL, I took it upon myself to fix it. To do this, I read the famous work of Sutton & Barto, the recent text of Krutz & Brunton, another text by Hu, and added several Medium articles readings to further expand my knowledge on RL.

After several weeks of learning and testing my own learning, I wrote the new RL material of the ACME program. Gymnasium maintains a lot of its old material but now has RL theory as well as an explanation on the RL framework and the divergence between model-free and model-based concepts. It mainly deals with model-free methods theory and use to help solve the famous Taxi environment of Gymnasium. Whereas, the last lab dealing with MDPs now focuses first on the mathematical theory learned and taught in our curriculum but then dives into the connection to the RL framework and model-based methods. Furthermore, it explains some of the challenges of working with both model-free and model-based methods as well as problems encountered overall within RL like the credit assignment problem. I hope to further expand this junior year curriculum into the senior labs specifically by implementing deep RL work and gradient descent methods. I will be working on this after completion of the fall semester of senior core of ACME once I have taken an grad-student course on RL offered by our CS department.

BYU Rocketry Club-Hybrid Rocket Research Team (Sep 2021-Aug 2024)

I joined the BYU Rocketry Club: Hybrid Rocket Research Team back in the fall of 2021. My friend Brennen invited me to come and at least see what the club was about. I am glad to say that the rest is history. I have always had a big affinity to the cosmos and intricacies of astronautics and the engineering and mathematics contained therein. Upon joining the club overseen by Dr. Gorrell, we were the recipients of a grant from the Utah-NASA Consortium of about $8,000 that was to be used to generate research for paraffin LOX hybrid fuel. We created a design for a 12ft rocket that would reach a target of 10,000 ft and have a maiden voyage in the summer of 2022. We (see below) were able to present our findings at the Utah-NASA Consortium 2022 Spring Conference and publish it in the Utah-NASA Consortium webpage .

I was working within the propulsion subteam to create the LOX dome, combustion chamber, fuel grain, and nozzle. I was tasked with implementing a design for the nozzle configurations that would enable us to reach a target exit mach number of 3.1. I studied the works of Gas Dynamics by Zucrow and learned more about the method characteristics employed for nozzle design. I was able to implement a Python3 script that would similate axisymmetric irrotational compressible flow given a throat area, radius of curvarture, desired exit mach number, and specific heat of the fluid. The script would output two solutions: 1. Rao nozzle in 3D (using Matplotlib) and 2. a txt, csv, excel file of the nozzle characteristic points that would then be used to create a CAD model in SolidWorks. I was later able to implement numerical methods for the transonic region analysis. The main goal of this was to better estimate the mach number achieved by the nozzle as well as better configure the mesh of the method of characteristics.
